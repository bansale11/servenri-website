The NRI's Complete Guide to Managing Indian Property from Abroad

Owning property in India while living abroad presents a unique set of challenges — from tenant disputes to legal compliance. This guide covers the full picture, so you can protect and grow your India assets without flying back for every problem.

Why Remote Property Management Fails

Most NRIs who own property in India have experienced at least one of these: a tenant who stopped paying rent and refused to leave, a building that fell into disrepair because no one coordinated repairs, a property tax notice that went ignored until penalties accumulated, or a relative acting as caretaker who stopped responding to calls.

The core problem is accountability without presence. When you are in California or Dubai, your leverage over a non-paying tenant in Jaipur is limited. Court proceedings in India require in-person representation or a valid Power of Attorney. Contractors quote inflated prices to absentee owners. Relatives acting informally as caretakers often cannot escalate issues or take legal action.

Managing Indian property from abroad is entirely possible — but it requires the right structure set up in advance, not improvised when something goes wrong.

Setting Up Your Management Structure

Before anything else, you need two things in place: a Power of Attorney (POA) and a clear mandate for whoever is managing your property.

Power of Attorney

A POA authorises a named person in India to act on your behalf — signing documents, appearing before authorities, and taking legal action if needed. Without one, you cannot do almost anything in India remotely. See our full POA guide for step-by-step instructions.

Who Should Manage Your Property?

You have three options:

  • A trusted family member or friend — low cost, but informal. They typically cannot take legal action, may not report issues proactively, and can create complications if the relationship sours.
  • A local property broker or agent — can find tenants and collect rent, but usually does not handle legal matters, government compliance, or maintenance coordination. Most are transactional, not management-oriented.
  • A professional NRI property management firm — handles the full scope: tenants, rent, maintenance, legal, compliance, and reporting. Higher cost, but appropriate for serious assets or situations that have already become complicated.

The key question: Is the person managing your property equipped to take formal action — send a legal notice, attend a court hearing, coordinate a contractor — without your physical presence? If not, you are one dispute away from a crisis.

Finding and Vetting Tenants

Tenant selection is the most consequential decision in property management. A good tenant pays on time, maintains the property, and leaves without drama when the lease ends. A problematic tenant can cost you months of lost rent and significant legal fees.

The Vetting Process

  • Identity verification: Aadhaar card, PAN card, and passport (if applicable). Cross-verify with the issuing documents, not just photocopies.
  • Employment and income: Salary slips (last 3 months), Form 16, or ITR for self-employed individuals. Rent should not exceed 30–35% of monthly income.
  • References: Previous landlord references are invaluable. Call them — don't just ask for the number.
  • Police verification: Legally required in most states. A formal police verification form is filed with the local police station. Many landlords skip this; don't.

The Rental Agreement

A properly drafted, registered rental agreement is your primary legal protection. Key provisions to include:

  • Clear rent amount, due date, and escalation clause (typically 5–10% per year)
  • Security deposit amount and conditions for deduction
  • Maintenance responsibilities split (minor repairs vs. major)
  • Notice period for termination by either party
  • Explicit clause permitting eviction for non-payment after specified notice

In most states, leases of 11 months or less do not require registration and are enforceable. For longer tenancies, registration at the Sub-Registrar office is required and provides stronger legal protection.

Important: Many landlords use unregistered 11-month agreements indefinitely, cycling them annually. While common, this means you cannot enforce certain rights in court. For high-value properties or longer tenancies, registration is worth the cost.

Rent Collection and Remittance

Receiving rent from your India property in your overseas account involves a specific process under FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act).

The Correct Banking Setup

Rental income earned in India must first go into an NRO (Non-Resident Ordinary) account in India. This is a mandatory requirement — rental income cannot be credited directly to an overseas account without first going through India's banking system.

From your NRO account, you can repatriate up to USD 1 million per financial year to your overseas account, after:

  • Paying applicable taxes (TDS on rent, if applicable)
  • Obtaining a certificate from a Chartered Accountant in Form 15CB
  • Filing Form 15CA online with the Income Tax portal

TDS on Rental Income

If your tenant is paying rent above ₹50,000 per month, they are required by law to deduct TDS at 31.2% (30% + 4% cess) on the rent paid to an NRI. This is a common source of confusion — the deduction obligation is on the tenant, not the landlord. Tenants who fail to deduct TDS can face penalties.

You can claim this TDS back against your India tax liability when filing an Indian income tax return.

Maintenance and Upkeep

Properties left unoccupied or poorly managed deteriorate rapidly in India's climate. Even occupied properties require active oversight to ensure maintenance is addressed promptly.

Setting Clear Responsibilities

The standard split for residential tenancies in India:

  • Tenant responsibility: Day-to-day upkeep, minor repairs (leaky taps, broken switches, pest control), interior painting at end of tenancy
  • Owner responsibility: Structural repairs, major plumbing or electrical work, exterior maintenance, appliances provided with the property

Ensure this is explicit in the agreement, not assumed. Vague agreements lead to disputes when significant repairs are needed.

Maintenance Reserves

Maintain a maintenance reserve of at least 1–2 months' rent equivalent in an India account accessible by your property manager. This allows them to authorise and pay for urgent repairs without waiting for international transfers, which can take days.

Regular Property Inspections

Quarterly inspections — with photo documentation sent to you — are the minimum standard for occupied properties. For vacant properties, monthly checks prevent encroachment, water damage, and deterioration that compounds over time.

Legal and Tax Compliance

Owning Indian property comes with ongoing legal and financial obligations that many NRIs are unaware of, or allow to lapse.

Property Tax

Annual property tax is payable to the local municipal body (Municipal Corporation, Gram Panchayat, etc.). Unpaid property tax accrues interest and can eventually result in the property being attached. Many NRIs discover years of unpaid tax when they attempt to sell.

Society Maintenance

If your property is in a housing society, monthly maintenance charges must be paid regardless of whether the property is occupied. Unpaid dues can result in the society restricting services (water, parking, lift access) and, in some cases, legal action.

Income Tax Filing in India

Rental income is taxable in India. NRIs are required to file Indian income tax returns if their India-source income exceeds the basic exemption limit. Even if it doesn't, filing can help you claim refunds of excess TDS deducted. Failure to file when required carries penalties.

FEMA Compliance

Holding property in India as an NRI is permitted, but certain transactions — particularly in agricultural land — require RBI approval. Selling property and repatriating proceeds also follows specific FEMA rules. See our repatriation guide for the full process.

Buying or Selling from Abroad

Both buying and selling Indian property as an NRI is possible without visiting India, provided you have a registered Power of Attorney in place. However, several factors must be managed carefully.

Selling NRI Property

The selling process involves: property valuation, finding a buyer, negotiating and finalising price, legal verification of title and encumbrances, preparing the sale deed, paying capital gains tax, obtaining the required forms (15CA/CB), executing the registered sale deed at the Sub-Registrar, and repatriating proceeds.

Capital Gains Tax: If held for more than 2 years, Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) apply at 20% with indexation benefit. If held for less, Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) are taxed at your applicable income tax slab. Buyers of NRI-owned property are required to deduct TDS at 20% of the sale value (not the gain) at source — this is a very common source of shock for NRI sellers.

Key point for sellers: The buyer must deduct TDS at 20% of the full sale value before paying you. If the property sells for ₹80 lakhs, ₹16 lakhs goes to the government as TDS. You can claim this back against actual tax liability when filing returns, but you must plan your cash flow accordingly.

Buying Property as an NRI

NRIs can purchase residential and commercial property in India freely (except agricultural land, plantation property, and farmhouses). Payment must be made from NRE or NRO accounts or through inward remittance — not from cash held in India. There is no limit on the number of properties an NRI can hold.

Staying Informed Without Being There

The greatest risk in remote property management is being the last to know when something goes wrong. A good management structure delivers proactive, documented reporting.

What Regular Reporting Should Include

  • Monthly: Rent receipt/payment confirmation, bank statement showing remittance to your account, any pending issues flagged
  • Quarterly: Property inspection report with photos, society dues status, utility bill status
  • Annually: Property tax paid receipt, TDS certificates, summary of annual income and expenses

If the person managing your property cannot consistently provide this level of documentation, you do not have meaningful visibility into what is happening to your asset.

When to Hire a Professional Management Firm

Consider professional property management if any of the following apply:

  • You have had a tenant dispute that required legal action, or fear you might
  • Your property has been vacant for more than 3–6 months
  • You cannot confirm your property tax and society dues are current
  • The person managing your property informally has become unresponsive or unreliable
  • You are considering selling and have no clear process to manage the transaction
  • You own multiple properties and the coordination burden is significant

The cost of professional management — typically 8–12% of monthly rent for an occupied property — is almost always less than the cost of a single dispute, a prolonged vacancy, or a mismanaged transaction.
